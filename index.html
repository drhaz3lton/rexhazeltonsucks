Rex Hazelton Sucks

What a whiner

Intro

Hello, World!

This is basically just an elongated journal entry written in a formal tone. Much of it has to do with my drinking, but maybe there are some decent thoughts it there. At least it will help explain about me a bit.

Character defects were at first something that I pretended I did not have or ran away from. At best they were something I woefully admitted existed, but that I hated and was embarrassed of, and therefore ignored in order to focus solely on my strengths. As long as I can remember I have had a deep desire to be anyone other than myself and have been deeply ashamed of my flaws. I do not have a single early (or late, for that matter) memory of being fine with who I am, but instead had a constant want to be whatever other person(s) I was currently fixated on. However, I have always been acutely aware of having talents beyond that of other people in some capacity, which may be the root of why I hated my flaws so much (I won't get into the dynamics of my childhood and the fixation on the shaming of my flaws and inordinate lauding of accomplishments by my parents here, that's another topic).

As a result, I would spend all of my childhood and adult energy trying to engeneder those attributes that I deemed worthy (strengths), the goal of which (I think) would be to gain happiness, glory, meaning, success or even achieve perfection (impossible) in some capacity. I would maximize my positive characteristics and therefore overcome/overshadow/distract from my defects. Maybe other people wouldn't even know those defects existed (save my numerous external ones). So, doubled-down on what I knew was good about me, in part, to shift my focus away from those imperfections. However, ignoring my defects did not make them go away; it instead provided the opportunity for them to slowly become entrenched and develop further as I continued to live solely focused on my innate strengths. Any time something negative was pointed out (by me or someone else), I could say "yeah, but look at this is over here" as if that made it dissapear - something I did increasingly as an adult. Every day that I pretended these issues weren't there or I ignored them, a piece of self hate grew in knowing that the problems had not magically resolved themselves and would continue. Both sides of me grew in tandem - for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction, yin and yang, etc. - so that as my strengths compounded, so did my weaknesses. They are both components of my innate person and therefore intertwined. I also eventually found that as I got farther away from a mistake I had made, or an opportunity, the size of my self loathing grew proportionately - I hated myself for mistakes I made when I was 18 far more at 30 than I did when I was 19. Negative thinking appears to compound within me. They say that time heals all wounds - but I dont think that applies to self inflicted ones. Every passing year was a reminder of those things that I had been unable to fix in the past, and their ferocity grew accordingly. It was a scratch in a record that got deeper and worse and more noticable every time it was played.

However, I didn't recognize the growth of my character defects, as opposed to noticing the psychological impact specific choices, because I had been consciously ignoring their existence. Mistakes would be made, but I wouldn't necessarily attribute them to a specific character defect making itself known, because I was trying to avoid seeing them. Instead attribute the mistake to myself a as whole - adding a tally to the growing list of evidence that I was simply "bad," rather behaving in a poor manner as a result of a personality trait embedded in me In addition, to me a defect at 30 was the same weakness that I had when I was ten; I was comparing the me today to the me when when I first discovered my problem - this "thing" that I have that is part of me. In my mind the defect was a stagnant and holistic idea, something that I either had or did not - an all or nothing proposition, rather than a trait that could evolve, and therefore cause impacts in greater and varying consequences over time. I wasn't a level two selfish at seven and a level nine selfish at 35, I was just selfish, full stop (but I would never tell YOU that I was selfish, because that part of me didn't actually exist, because it's bad and I'm good). In reality my defects had matured significantly over time - every instance in which I fell victim to my lesser traits, the stronger the defect got and the more I had to pretend I was fine and act as though it wasn't there. Thus began a process wherein I had to become detached from myself. I was psychologcally splitting myself between the reality of who I actually was and the person that I was pretending to be. Given my childhood, slipping into a version wherein I say one thing while thinking something else came very easily to me (some would call it lying, some would call it surviving - I dont think there is a difference here). By creating this dual existence, I was able to make sense of how I felt by assigning different thought patterns or behaviors to different consciousnesses (personalities, if you will). It enabled me to have a holistic external me the could be judged by others based on worldly rules and a holistic internal me that operated within my own personal realm.

Over time, my weaknesses grew to be as large as my strengths because no mental or emotional energy was being expended to combat them in my external world. Lots of time was spent focusing on them internally, but no one knew about them there - instead they just stewed around and waited until they could boil over. I would often tell myself that I was not feeling a certain way, or more precisely COULD not feel a certain way, because the external version of me did not have the capacity to feels those things - the external me had only positive attributes. As a result, the defects and associated emotions began to overtake me seemingly out of nowhere and in far greater force - because saying something does not make it true (the greatest trick the devil ever pulled was convincing the world he didn't exist). Further, these character defects were increasingly putting themselves in direct opposition to my goals, both personal and professional - not something to simply be ignored. My character defects began causing me immense psychic pain, not only because they highighlighted the flaws that I desperately wanted to be rid of, but also because I was forcing myself to psychologically split myself in two: the person who I actually was and the person that I pretended to be and told myself I was - a person for which these flaws did not matter (or didn't actually exist). I had first begun to entirely detach from who I was and therefore detach from reality, and as a result my entire process of thinking had to shift into this duality so that I was able survive once. In the end I was making decisions based upon and for a person that did not exist - something I will talk about more later.

When I first discovered alcohol, it provided me with relief from this psychological pain (emphasis on discover). Alcohol was something that I, on my own, found and which provided me with relief from pain which I alone could understand. No other person could comprehend the pain that I was feeling because it was entirely unique to me, caused and felt by me, for which I had no remedy. Therefore, no one could also know the ecstasy of suddenly being relieved of that pain - to describe the feeling of being able to walk upright after being burdened by countless tons of physic weight laboring every effort for your entire life. A whole new world opened up. There are so few moments in life in which a person truly discovers a new thing or idea that is deeply personal, rather than is learned about second hand from someone else - a truly orignal feeling and thought that stems from the depths of your being. I was lost, but now am found, was blind but now I see. Discovering a music is something that can do this, love can as well - but those are both discoveries that take you from a state of normalcy into a state of euphoria - this is a single reality shift. Alcohol was something entirely different - it was not only able to unbridle me from the pain (shift one), but could then take me from (perceived) normalcy and into a state of euphoria - one in which I cognitively no longer had the desire nor even capacity to think about my pain (shift two). Alcohol did not build upon something else and led me to a discovery - it provided me with a solution to a problem that I didn't even know I had. I was at one end of the emotional spectrum and was instantaneously teleported to its exact opposi

te, and it took me years to understand that is what had happened; I was born anew. Prior to that moment there was just existence that included constant anguish - after that moment I knew that there was a way that I could live with that removed, however fleeting a feeling. There is nothing else I can think of other than life and death that can rapidly enable this degree of change.

As a result, this idea became immediately a deeply entrenched belief and idea within myself: alcohol can give me relief from the mental pain I continually feel as a consequence of being aware of my own fallibility, which is in essence my humanity (whether I knew that or not). In other words, alcohol meant that I could escape from being me, the one thing that I had always wanted. Conversely, it meant that anytime that I was unable to relieve this pain, it was multiplied because I knew that there was a mechanism by which it could be, but I was being prevented from doing so - someone or something was standing in my way and was therefore to be hated. This psycholoical pain was further exacerbated by the fact that I was seeking a remedy for something that I was telling myself didn't exist - the power that my defects had over me (and potentially the defects themselves). I was running away from something that I could not escape (my own flaws/self), but alcohol was able to temporarily let me hide and catch my breath. During those times of relief I felt that there was a capacity to fully escape from the feeling of being myself (oblivion), wherein could I fully live in a world where in I did not have any flaws (or pain). The logical conclusion of this is a world in which in do not actually exist, am able to feel unending joy, which I believe it called heaven. During my active drinking I simply said "I'm just trying to drink enough to not think thoughts and not feel feelings," which is a little less romantic. This brings me to a discussion about drugs and alcohol being a psychological replacement for spirituality (albeit weak and unsatisfactory version), though I will save that for

another day - but they both have the same end goal.

Escaping this pain through by existing in a mental plane detached from reality became my main pursuit - it became the meaning of life for me (and life is suffering, as any religion or philosophy will attest). Therefore escape became the point of life. This does not mean that I immediately started consuming alcohol at all times, rather that everything I did was oriented around constructing a time wherein I could imbibe at any level pleased - and that that time was sacred to me. In this mental realm I could exist in the manner I pleased. But over time, my need to take this mental vacation grew more and more time consuming. At first it was occasional, but as my problems grew a greater respite was needed. At some point I crossed a barrier in which I was no longer taking a break for reality to gain relief, but I was taking a break from relief in order to do just enough living to enable my next break. This alternate reality increasingly began to bleed into my real life, and eventually would overtake it. I started to make decisions for the person who lived in the real world (me) as though he was that "idealized" person, the one who did not feel the pain he endured, did not have the attributed flaws and did not face any of the consequences - and that's because that's where I was spending most of my time. At this point, the shift had come full circle - I had first created a distinct alternate reality which was self contained, now but was no longer able distinguish betwern the two. When I started, the real me did things to let the other me have fun sometimes. In the end, the other me was making the real me do things in life so that he could do whatever he wanted. Instead of drinking

to stop caring about things I didn't like about myself, I started to drink in order to do the things in real life that only the person who existed in my alternate reality would do, because consequences of his mistakes did not matter. I began to do things that I knew were intrinsically opposed to everything I believed, but when I drank it wasn't me doing those things - it was the version of me I had created and existed only in an alternate reality of my creation. I would would think of something I knew was wrong and that I would never do, but knew that this other version of me could and would be ok with it, and once I drank that version of me could engage in any manner of activities. But when I awoke it me who was left to deal with the mess I had created and the accompanying pain, of which I had no choice but to escape from - because that was the whole point of life. Alcohol gave me the permission to let go of the reigns of my subconscious, knowing full well the darkness that was contained inside. And so, the death spiral of drinking to escape was enacted, unleashing my alternate consciousness upon the world which would wreak havoc upon me until I regained consciousness, the pain of which I would then realize and could only escape through more drinking, and the cycle continues until a force greater than myself puts an end to it.

Another part of this psychic split stems from one of my character defects: as long as I can remember, my favorite thing has been getting away with something. Whether large or small, I have been addicted to the rush of lying or pulling a fast one on somebody - that somehow I got more than I deserved or figured out a way to get what I was told I deserved without putting the necessary effort in. As a child, lying or stealing was enough to fill this gap. I took to a politically rebellious philosophy as a matter of rebellion - identifying this trait in other people, especially those in power and decrying its loathsomeness, while participating in it myself. It's not lost on me that the people shouting most loudly about how things arent fair in kne place are the people who are most efficiently trying to undermine fairness somewhere else. In my youth I had plenty of people to work over - my parents, my teachers, my church, other kids. But over time there were fewer people to try to pull things on, I could slack off at work and still get paid, but my world had become smaller and the people who were available to lie to were increasingly just the people that were close to me and I cared about. But since I refused to address this character defect, I would just keep giving into it and think of it as a white lie or a small guilty pleasure. I also started drinking during events where I should have been sober and while I worked - because that filled my need to get away with something. Sure, I'll do work for you, but I am going to be drunk while I do it and you won't be the wiser. The idea that drinking is a form of rebellion and non-confirmism is so misguided. Doing something almost everyo

ne else does (albeit in moderation), but that only really hurts you as a mechanism to show your resentment to people or the world is absurd. Further, as I grew older the lies turned into things that could really hurt people emotionally, financially or physically - the consequences to me and everyone around me grew. At the same time, my world was growing smaller and smaller as I drank more and more, and eventually it was only my family or my partner who I could lie to or hide from. In the end, it left only me - so then who could I get away with stuff against. Countless times I remember my dual consciousness facing myself in the mirror, one version telling myself that I shouldnt do something, and the other version saying that I could do it and that somehow I wouldnt find out (ie. care). All that was left was me trying to pull a fast one on myself, saying that I could drink without consequences and get away with it, but I was always there to find out that I was wrong. I had imploded.

Long ago I came to the realization that I was an alcoholic, but only recently realized that ever since I discovered alcohol, I had been living in a constant state of waiting until I could drink again. In the last years of my drinking it was abundantly clear that was the case, but for most of it. I would go to work, play sports, spend time with family and friends, etc. - but this was all biding my time knowing that I would eventually be able to drink once more. At the time it was happening I believed it was because drinking meant that it was time to really have fun. I could have "fun" sober, but REAL fun started once alcohol entered. It was pleasure on overdrive. I just didn't realize that due to the psychological turmoil and relief that I described above, and the different physiological and neurological effects alcohol has on me compared to others, how much more "fun" drinking was for me than for other people. It never occured to me that other people didn't always make their last drink of the night be their strongest, or that other people didn't have more energy after six hours of drinking than they did when they started. That's just how it always was for me. But I digress.

Over time, alcohol played an increasingly larger role partly because it made all the mundane events into fun events. But eventually it made fun events mundane, and mundane events horrible. That is because I had it all backwards - I had not been seeking fun, I had been seeking relief from pain, pain that had been increasingly growing as my character defects enlarged in conjunction with my denial of those very defects. As I continually sought more and more relief from pain, under the guise of seeking more and more fun (work hard, play hard, right?) I began to lose control over not only my defects, but my strengths as well. The things that I used as a pillar to stand myself up upon aqnd hold me up against my defects began to waiver. I was no longer focusing on development of my strengths and avoiding my weaknesses, I was focusing on my ability to damp down the psychological pain my own character flaws were causing me - I was no longer strong enough to do both things. Once I began to focus on the avoidance of the past (defects) I was no longer looking towards the future (strengths). That was the beginning of the end. What I didn't realize was that I am first and foremost an alcoholic

above all else. I was not a student than drank alcholically, or a boyfriend who drank alcohically, or a consultant who drank alcoholically; I was an alcoholic who went to school, and an alcoholic who dated girls and an alcoholic that went to work. Alcohol was my top priority, everything else was just biding time til my next drink - I just didn't know that is what I was doing, I just thought I was living.

I eventually reached a point at which I was been forced to stand squarely focused on my character flaws because they had overwhelmed me. This was not my idea, but I also always knew it was coming. I have always felt deep down that I was going to push and push and push until either I broke or found out that I was a demigod. I thought that's how champions were made, playing through the pain and ignoring the warning signs. I always said that I binge life - taking on or taking in more than I should - in order to emerge as somehow heroic or larger than life in the end. But, that isn't how emotional success works; my life cracked and everything came pouring out. I was left to with nothing to do but see what was left there. When I first came to really look at it, to identify and recognize this mess that was my character defects and my life, my instinct was to be aghast and quickly to try and run away, insist they weren't there or simply become overwhelmed by them. I did so because that is what I had always done. Of course I would be driven to that route of action - in the past every time I thought about my defects, it caused me psychological pain. I learned that thinking about those things wa

s a death sentence. In fact, thinking about these defects was exactly what got me here in the first place, why would I do that now? Wouldn't focusing my attention onto a new person, place, thing or idea be a much more effective plan? A fresh canvas onto which I can start anew and leave all this mess behind? An all new me can emerge like a pheonix from the ashes, squeaky clean and ready to face the day. That could work, if only that mess wasn't the sum total of who I am and everything I know and that guy follows me wherever I go. As a result, I made multiple futile attempts at starting a new life with the best of intentions, but time and time again the old me would catch up and bring along my defects, and eventually my thinking and behavior would change back. How could it not? Those ideas were the only way that I knew how to do live that had substance - it may have been wrong, but at least it was familiar and I generally knew what the outcome would be - everything else I had been doing was difficult, unknown and unproven. My wrong choices were what got me here, but he same person also made all the decisions that worked out positively in my life - I cant pick and choose which parts of me that I want to keep.

But gradually over time I began to see that if taken in small chunks, I could begin to dissect my character defects, assess and address them. Historically, I would dwell on past mistakes and flaws, beat myself up over them and hate myself. All this was done, in theory, to teach myself not to do them again and maybe somehow that would correct the past too. But, of course, I repeated the same mistakes again and again, hating myself all the more. Why would I then dwell on the memories? To teach myself better? To cause myself pain? (learning is often painful, but pain doesn't beget knowledge) No, I do not think so. I think it is because subconsciously I knew that I had not actually LEARNED from the mistakes. All I had done was committ an error and then try to ignore it as fast as possible, dump it into some deep mental well and clean up any evidence of its existence. I of course knew I had done it, but could try and live as though I didn't and hoped no one would ever find out (and heaven help you if you did and confronted me with my mistake - don't you know that I have been berating myself to hell ever since it happened with every waking moment? You think I need help with this? Don't you kn

ow that I am currently at the bottom of a well drowning in every other mistake I have ever made in addition to this new bucket you just poured onto me? Oh I see, you are trying to kill me! Well buckle up...). Despite my best efforts, my sober subconscious would consistently throw my mistake back up into my thought process as if to say "Hey, you haven't resolved this problem yet. I am a brain and what I do is solve problems, but you keep on leaving this issue unresolved and it can't go away until it is, that's why I am here." Therefore, what I have to do is entirely unpack each of these defects and errors, pull them apart and examine every last detail as to what happened and why - and then I can ACTUALLY learn what happened and figure out what went wrong, what I did that was erroneous and figured out how I code have behaved differently. I can actually extract wisdom from these events to inform all kinds of situations. What I saw as a fatal flaw - my inability to forget my problems, is actually just my mind getting my attention in order to fix something. Not to ruminate on the past, but to resolve the problem and move past them. Problem solving is a strength; I checked my resume and it does not say "Strengths: Adept at ignoring glaring issues and denying reality until everything falls apart and I am forced to reconcile everything under great duress." But resolving problems takes action and effort, not just thinking.

As a result, I came to view my character defects as an opportunity for personal growth, that each defect was something that I could work on in order to bring the entirety of my being into alignment with an idealized version of myself (lets call it God, something perfect yet unattainable, but something I can understand and desire to emulate). With each small step that I take to improve my weaker traits, or to reduce the power that those traits have over me, I was able to see that my imperfections were actually the opportunity for me to pursue self actualization and become. I cannot overcome me weaknesses by building up my strengths, that just leaves me in continually building up my defenses in a war or good versus evil waiting for evil to strike. But the fight is just me versus myself - I have to attack the negative characteristics in order to overcome them, not simply hope to keep them at bay. The very things that I saw as a hindrance to my existence were the things that were actually pointing me to how I could become a better person. They were showing me how I could grow and become more comfortable with and proud of myself - how I could escape the mental pain that had been overwhelming me. Fo

r so long I had been thinking about defects and problems the wrong way - you don't fix a character defect by ignoring it or just not behaving in that manner, you fix it by identifying the trait and then actively choosing the do its opposite. Over time the opposite behavior will become your default reaction and supplant the negative trait. By taking every day and identifying these defects and desiring to do their oppsoite, I am able to address them and identify ways that can gradually bring me closer to that idealized version of myself - closer to God (both in comparison to and relationship with). Satisfaction and purpose are derived from taking on challenges and giving my best effort to meet those obstacles. The great satisfaction I have felt when I used my strengths to achieve something is the exact same satisfaction that I feel when I do something that I know is in opposition to my innate negative traits - something that is difficult to do. Looking for the easy way around something does not lead to satisfaction - the effort ascribed is intrinsic to its value. I tried to take the easy way around my defects by ignoring them - get away with having them. But my character defects are actually showing me the pathway to derive meaning in my life by taking responsibility for them.

This brought me to the eternal question of why evil exists; why God allows evil into the world. I think the answer is that without evil, there cannot be virtue. If everyone one is good, then no one is good, we all just are. Who is more virtuous, the man who only knows how to be good, or the man who knows he is evil and chooses every day not to be? Clearly it is the latter, as he is the one who made the virtuous choice; the former simply acted as a fish swimming in water - it was an action of the lower brain and without thought. It is doesn't require the very part of the brain that makes us human! Therefore, every day that I wake up and recognize that I am flawed and take steps to act in a manner that directly opposes my defects, I am pursuing virtue. While many of my defects may not be things that are considered "evil," they are still ideas that present me with an option as to pursue Godliness or not - an option to chose things that bring me more into alignment who I want to be, someone who lives in pursuit of virtue and meaning.

What this inherently means, then, is that without my character defects, I would be unable to pursue a life that is both virtuous and meaningful. Without them, I could live a life that is in alignment with God, but this would not be a choice, thereby negating the very thing that makes us human (to err is human, I suppose). Living in a world without personal defects would foreseeably allow me to derive happiness, but it would not allow me to derive meaning, because I must have a choice in order to be virtuous.

That means that my character defects are, in actuality, the most valuable part of who I am. Understanding my flaws and actively pursuing their minimization (and ideally, elimination) is how I derive purpose and value. In short, that is the purpose of life. I have often heard that the meaning of life is to reduce the amount of pain and suffering in your world. The characteristics that are attributed to virtue, ie. goodness, kindness, patience, are the things that reduce suffering and are also the very things that hel

p me work aginst my character defects. Therefore, by engaging in activities that minimize the pain of others around me, I am actively strengthening my own ability to combat the mental anguish I have always been trying to escape. The meaning and purpose of life seem to be hidden within my own character defects. Without my character defects, I cannot find meaning.

This seems to be the essence of how backwards my thinking was. For much of my life I have been consumed with the idea of finding a purpose, but solely focused on external ideas (people, places, things, concepts) as a mechanism to find that purpose and meaning. At the same time, I was wholeheartedly pursuing the avoidance of pain (my substitute meaning of life as stated earlier), which stemmed from me wanting to escape my character defects. But now I am able to see that facing my character defects is the mechanism by whi

ch I derive that meaning of which I had been seeking. Therefore, everything I had been doing in pursuit of happiness and purpose had, in fact, been the exact opposite of what I should have done. My thinking was backwards, yet again. The way that I have evolved to operate over the past 30 years based upon my own logic has been entirely wrong. Shit. I guess that means I should listen to other people for a while.
